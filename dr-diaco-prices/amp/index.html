Fat transfer breast augmentation can be a good alternative to breast implants.

Nonsurgical breast enhancement is nearly impossible. Some women find that their breasts increase in size after starting on oral birth control pills or gaining weight. Pills and creams offer little to no evidence of breast enhancement.

However, if you are seeking an alternative to breast implants, you may be a good candidate for fat transfer breast augmentation. This is a two-step procedure that involves harvesting fat cells from another part of your body (usually the hips) and transferring the cells to your breasts for a modest increase in bust size.

Fat transfer breast augmentation is best for women seeking a subtle size increase. If you’re interested in a more pronounced augmentation, you should consider the benefits of breast implants, as no alternative can currently produce the same results.

How to decide what style breast implant is best for me.

The dimensions of the implant are the most important. Your surgeon should measure your breast and chest width. Based on this, there are several implants that will fit your frame, including different styles such as high or moderate profile.

Try on the different styles in the sizes that fit your frame and look in a full length mirror, including from the side, to see which you like better. Also put on different types of tops and look.

In my experience, very few first time augmentation patients want high profile implants. Also, high profile implants will tend to stretch your tissues more over time and they project a lot. It is usually a much more “dramatic” look.

Spending the time pre-operatively to select the right implant for you is very important and your plastic surgeon and his/her office staff should be happy to help you with this.
Pain after a breast augmentation

You are absolutely right – pain is a very subjective and individualized “experience.” Many women compare the pain of breast augmentation to the sensation when their milk first came in – of feeing engorged. That being said, in my experience almost all patients are able to go back to a desk job no later than 6 days post-operatively.

To decrease post-operative pain I inject a long acting anesthetic to keep the patient as comfortable as possible. Additionally, in circumstances where patients wish to minimize their use of narcotics I will also prescribe both Celebrex (a cox-2 inhibitor – kind of like a super duper Advil) as well as Lyrica.
