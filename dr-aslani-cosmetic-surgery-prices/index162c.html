Saline vs regular silicone vs anatomical silicone

Currently we have three choices of implants in the US.

1: regular cohesive gel implants, there are the most commonly used implants in the US. Give you the more natural feel, rippling is reduced. Chances of rupture is small but only way to determine this occurrence is with specialized x-rays, FDA recommend MRI at year 3 and every 2 years after that.

2: highly cohesive silicone implants also known as anatomical shaped implants or “gummy bear”, these are the most commonly used implants in Europe.

A significant number of surgeons, believe these implants give you the most natural look, however they feel harder than the regular silicone. Same MRI studies, FDA recommendations.

3: saline implants, now less commonly used in the US, show rippling more, specially in thinner patients, feel harder due to easier transmission of force by fluids, specially when overfilled. Ruptures are diagnosed easier.

Smaller incision are possible. In regards to implant cost (just the actual implant, not the surgery, anesthesia and operating room costs) saline cost the less, regular gel in the middle and anatomical implant cost the most.

Patients like you, will look good with any type of implants, but the ultimate decision is personal and you will have to make it, hopefully with the guidance of a board certified plastic surgeon, preferably a member of the American Society for Aesthetic Plastic Surgery.

Bigger breast implant size is not always better

Americans love to supersize. Big cars, supersized fries, the super big gulp. But bigger is not better when it comes to breast implants. I hear you want large implants. I understand when having a AA cup your whole life, when you do breast implants you really want to see a difference.
They cost the same. Isn’t bigger better? Why did your doctor talk you out of a big size?

1. When you are thin, you have thin tissue cover over the implant, so you will droop, bottom out, and see implant abnormalities.
2. If you go to a super large size, even if we put you in a high profile breast implant, it will be too wide. You will hit your breast when doing sports and normal activities.
3. The weight of the implant will cause you to droop.
4. The weight of the implant can cause back and neck pain and the issues my breast reduction patients come in to treat.
5. They will look fake.

Not pretty. I fix many of these down the road. I don’t put them in. If you take a long term view, then aim for the long haul to have pretty breasts. If you do large ones now, you will downsize later, have back pain, and likely need a lift.
Rippling is a fairly common risk factor of implants and is one of the reasons that plastic surgeons were all happy to see silicone implants come back on the market. Silicone gel implants are less likely to ripple than saline implants.

Also, thin patients tend to have more problems with rippling than patients who are heavier or who have more breast tissue to begin with.

In most patients, the rippling is most significant in the lower, outside part of the breast where there is less breast, muscle and skin to cover the implant.

This is a question you should discuss with your plastic surgeon when you meet with them so that they can give you an answer that is specific to you. Overall, this is usually not a big problem after breast augmentation surgery.

Breasts getting smaller a year after implants

A decrease in the size of your breasts can occur for many reasons. Immediately after augmentation, your breasts will be swollen, and you may notice a decrease in size over a span of several months.

However, after a year, it may be a combination of factors.

1. Weight loss is commonly manifested in the breasts before other places in the body, and thus decrease the size of your breasts.
2. Rupture of the implants may have occurred
3. If you gain weight, your implants may appear smaller as you get bigger 4. Capsular contracture may give the appearance of smaller implants as the scar tissue contracts and elevates the implant

The bottom line is to have your implants evaluated by your plastic surgeon.

Pregnancy and Breast Augmentation

Most women are able to breast feed after having breast augmentation. However, pregnancy can change a woman’s body, including breast size and shape, even though it doesn’t impact the implant itself. Surgeries can be performed after pregnancy to improve or correct these changes to the breast.
