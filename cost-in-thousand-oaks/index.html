  1. The type of implant
  2. The type of facility in which the surgery is performed
  3. The potential need for a lift along with the implantation.

Implants come in various types, ranging in price from the least expensive, which is saline, to the most expensive, which is the form-stable, AKA gummy bear implants, with the gel-filled (sometimes called “silicone implants) in the middle.

Remember, breast implants in Thousand Oaks usually last for many years, so don’t choose saline if that’s not what you truly want. Both gel-filled and form-stable implants are closer to the feel of real breast tissue compared with saline implants. The type of facility affects the price also. Many patients are surprised to find that they would benefit from a breast lift along with implants. Drooping breasts cannot be remedied with an implant alone. If you have drooping breasts due to genetics, pregnancy, breastfeeding or aging, you may need a breast lift, which can add to the price but which will give patients with breast drooping (called ptosis) a much superior result.

When you call around to various plastic surgery offices, keep in mind that many won’t give prices over the telephone, which is in your favor. The best size for your breast implant is the size that will achieve YOUR goals. Sizing is very subjective. There is simply not one size that is “right” for any body type, but rather a range of sizes that can work based on a patient’s anatomical dimensions.

Measurements are important, especially in regards to implant diameters (there are various “profiles” of implants at any given size, which means a specific volume implant will be available in different widths).

Your surgeon should measure your dimensions to help determine which implants will fit properly in your breasts.

In my practice, I provide patients with implant sizers which can be placed within a bra during the consultation to help the patient determine the implant volume that will achieve their goals.

I will counsel them on whether the implant volume seems appropriate for those goals. I find that this preoperative sizing is extremely valuable in achieving the results that the patient is looking for.

It is important to keep in mind that there is a limit to how large you can go. At a certain point, an implant can be too large for your body, and this can increase the risk for complications. How big is too big will depend on each person’s anatomy. (Robert Wilke, MD, Edina Plastic Surgeon)

Optimum breast implant sizing

There is no “cookie-cutter” approach to sizing for breast implants in Thousand Oaks to achieve a “natural” look. Because natural breasts have significant variability, dependng on native breast volume, shape, nipple position, descent (or “sag”), and symmetry issues, what one patient considers “natural” may differ from another patient’s perception.

It is therefore very important to try to explain your cosmetic goals to your plastic surgeon during your consultation(s). Pictures of models illustrating your ideal breast appearance can be helpful, but be careful not to assume that there is any guarantee of getting the exact same look.

Many patients who end up with an “unnatural” look do so because of poor communication regarding their goals preoperatively. But there are also intraoperative and some postoperative factors that can contribute to the appearance. The dissection of the implant pocket by the surgeon is the most “controllable” factor in determining the final appearance.

Using overly large implants for the patient’s chest wall measurements can contribute to an unnatural outcome. In addition, the placement of implants over the muscle may make them more visible and detectable, particularly in patients with relatively small preoperative breast volume. The surgeon must take care to make a well designed pocket for the implant – not too spacious, and not too tight – and this step is often aided by careful preoperative marking and review of photographs. Some surgeons advise their patients to massage their implants after surgery to help them “drop” into the pocket, as it is common for the chest muscle to go into some spasm early after surgery, which can push the implants up initially. Check with your surgeon regarding his/her posteropative protocols. In short, there is no simple way to ensure a “natural” look, but these factors may help. Finally, a very important factor is to make sure your plastic surgeon is board certified by the American Board of Plastic Surgery. (Tim A. Sayed, MD, FACS, Boca Raton Plastic Surgeon)

Breast implant replacement

I hear this question often and I am not surprised that you are confused. There are a lot of different opinions out there. I frequently hear that some doctors tell their patients that they need to replace their implants every 10 years.

I am not sure where this number comes from. My philosophy (and what I plan to do myself!) is “If it ain’t broke. Don’t fix it” or using better grammer.” If they don’t bother you, don’t bother them”.

I do not think that anything dangerous happens at 10 years that would require you to replace your implants. Rupture rates do increase over time but are still very low at 10 years.

If you are happy with the appearance and overall feel of your breasts then there is no reason to have surgery at a specific time. Reasons to consider replacing your implants might be:

  1. You wish to be larger or smaller
  2. You have a ruptured implant
  3. You have developed a capsular contracture which has caused uneven appearance
  4. Your body has changed since you had implants put in and current breast appearance needs adjustment (most common reasons for body change are pregnancy, weight loss or weight gain)

Whether you have breast implants or not, I recommend that you do monthly self breast exams and have a yearly medical breast exam. If you are over 40 I recommend that you get a yearly mammogram as well. If you do have implants be sure to inform your mammographer (they will figure it out anyway!) so that a few special views can be done to get the best information form the test.

It is not a bad idea to see your plastic surgeon every year or so.
