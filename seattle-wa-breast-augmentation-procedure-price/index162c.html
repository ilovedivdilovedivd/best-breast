Periareolar “lift” – limited utility

After seeing your breasts, I strongly discourage you from considering the periareolar lift. In my opinion, the periareolar mastopexy is an operation that has very limited application. It is useful for reducing areola size and for slightly moving the areola position.

It tends to flatten the breast rather than lifting it, and can result in very abnormal appearing areolas. Think about this: areola skin is thin and soft, similar to eyelid skin. Breast skin is thicker, similar to cheek skin.

The periareolar mastopexy is performed by removing a dough-nut of skin from around the areola, and “purse-stringing” the breast skin in towards the nipple. Even if a reinforcing permanent stitch is used to hold the thick breast skin inward, often the breast skin “wins” and stretches the areola out to strange proportions.
(This is much more likely to happen if you add the stress of an implant behind it!) To get the best result, you should stage your operations – perform a lift first and let the scars heal without the tension caused by an implant (this will give you the best looking scars).

Once they have healed (3-6 months), you can safely place a proportionately large implant and achieve the perky look you are searching for.

Whenever you have an incision, you will have a scar. However, advanced techniques and the use of scar treatment programs can leave you with very discrete scars that are hardly recognizable. My preferred method for breast augmentation is through the areola.

My advanced techniques also allow for less pressure on the incision site, which helps reduce the thickness of the scar as well. Transaxillary breast augmentation (through the armpit) is also a popular choice for patients because no scars are left on the breast at all, although healing time is slightly longer because the implant has to travel a further distance.

Lots of options for tuberous breasts

While it sounds like you are describing a tuberous breast problem, it is a diagnosis that has to be made by examining your breasts properly. Women with tuberous breasts often have a combination of large areolas compared to the rest of their breast size, droopiness, high inframammary fold (where the breast gland meets the lower chest) and poorly developed lower pole of the breast gland.
Tuberous breasts can be small or big! The goal of surgery is to achieve symmetry between the breasts using incisions that release the constriction ring of fascia that gives the breast its odd shape. After that, breast implants in Seattle are used to shape and increase the size of smaller breasts OR a breast lift/reduction can be done for larger breasts.

In both surgical options the nipple is brought to a higher position and the breast gland and inframmamary fold are lowered.
